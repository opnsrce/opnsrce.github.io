For the past five years of my life, I&#8217;ve been a web developer. I&#8217;ve written probably a million lines of code and have developed at least 5 distinct medium to large-scale web applications or sites. Despite my accomplishments, whenever I get around other web professionals I feel like I don&#8217;t belong. Couple this with the quality of both the attendees and speakers at An Event Apart in Seattle, and I felt downright stupid. Everywhere I went, I saw lanyards containing some of the biggest names in the industry: Facebook, Microsoft, PageRank, and THQ. In this sea of giants, of people who have changed the face of the web, who was I? What had I accomplished? Did I really deserve to be here, or was my attendance simply a matter of paying for a ticket? Am I a fraud? That last question was the one I had to answer if I was going to truly gain anything from this conference.<!--more-->

## The Typkit Pre-conference Party

Pre-conference parties are pretty standard fare: You show up, drink free booze on the sponsor&#8217;s dime, and schmooze about the room. For me, this event would set the tone of the rest of the conference. For starters, it introduced me to a lot of really talented people. But one in particular hard words of wisdom that I&#8217;ll be remembering for years to come: Kevin Jennings.

As I set down with [Kevin](http://www.kevinjenningsdesign.com/), [Brandon](http://blog.fallenrayne.net/), and [Beth](http://www.eeblet.com/) I asked &#8220;How do you get over the feeling that you&#8217;re a fraud?&#8221; After a moment of pondering, Kevin responded (paraphrased):

> The scariest thing about this industry is promising to do something you&#8217;ve never done before: a new type of design or maybe an application. But the reality is, you have all the skills to build what they&#8217;re asking for, you&#8217;ve just never thought to combine them in that particular way.

Those words would stick with me the rest of the conference. What Kevin said made me step back and re-visit all the opportunities I had turned down because the client asked me to do something I&#8217;d never done before. In reality, I had the experience to do what they wanted, I&#8217;d just never combined my skills in that particular fashion. I think of it like cooking: a meal is just a combination of ingredients prepared with a few basic skills. The greatest ability of any professional chef, isn&#8217;t having a library of recipes stored away in the back of their mind, it&#8217;s being able to break down any new recipe into a series of skills they already know and having the confidence to combine those skills to make something beautiful.

## A Chance Encounter With Jeffrey Zeldman

One the memorable moments of the AEA conference actually occurred before it began. As I was getting ready to leave the registration area, the elevator doors opened and out stepped Jeffrey Zeldman. Now, for those of you who don&#8217;t know, Zeldman is a personal hero of mine. His work is not only amazing, but his contribution to my education as a web developer has given me the skills I use every day to provide for my family. And here he was, right in front of me. What could I possibly say to this man? What eloquent words of appreciation could I speak in order to thank him for everything he&#8217;s done? Well, I have no clue because the first words out of my mouth were &#8220;Oh my God! You&#8217;re Jeffrey Zeldman&#8221;. Classy, Levi. Classy. As if he was going to stumble back in surprise and say &#8220;By God! You&#8217;re absolutely right!&#8221;. Thankfully, he just smiled, shook my hand, and asked if he could take my [picture](http://www.flickr.com/photos/zeldman/5565968175/) (presumably for some sort of restraining order).

## Revelations

Throughout the first and into the second day of the conference, I still had that nagging question in the back of my brain: Am I a fraud? Despite the amazing speeches, the inspiring stories and, the wonderful people, I still felt like I didn&#8217;t measure up. This all changed after Tom Coates wrapped up his speech with this (paraphrased):

> We collectively connect more things everyday. We are the road-builders of the future. The planet and everything on it is our canvas and our brush.

The answer to my question didn&#8217;t lie with Jeffrey, Sarah Parmenter, or Tom Coates. The answer was in the eyes of every person I encountered at the conference. Every time I got a complete stranger interested my work, or inspired another developer to research some new technology; every time I put a smile on someone&#8217;s face and got them excited about the what the web would someday become, I became not only a more genuine web developer, but a more genuine person.

We&#8217;re _all_ making a difference. It doesn&#8217;t matter how long you&#8217;ve been in this industry or what you&#8217;ve accomlished. As long as you&#8217;re passionate about what you&#8217;re doing, you belong here. You don&#8217;t have to be as CSS-savvy as Eric Meyer or as innovative as [Sarah Parmenter](http://www.youknowhodesign.com). As long as you care about what you&#8217;re doing and aren&#8217;t afraid to learn from your failures, you&#8217;re as genuine as they come.
